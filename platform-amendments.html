Amendment I · New Section 2.15

Property Taxation

The Motion

Amend the Montana Libertarian Party Platform by adding a new Section 2.15 (Property Taxation), to follow Section 2.14, reading in full as follows:

Perpetual taxation of real property is incompatible with genuine ownership. When the state retains the power to seize a home for unpaid taxes, what appears to be ownership is in substance a tenancy — the owner pays the state rent in perpetuity, regardless of whether the property generates income, and forfeits everything if unable to pay. A home is not truly owned if it can be taken for failure to pay an annually escalating tax on its appraised value.

The Montana Libertarian Party therefore calls for the eventual abolition of property taxation in Montana, consistent with Section 2.4. As intermediate measures we support: constitutional limits on annual assessment increases; uniform treatment of similarly situated properties, without preferences or penalties based on ownership duration or other factors unrelated to the property itself; and transparent assessment, mill-levy, and appeal processes that ordinary Montanans can navigate without paid representation.

We reject the framing that property taxes are necessary to fund local government. Services currently funded through property taxes can be provided through user fees, voluntary associations, private contracts, and consumption-based mechanisms that do not place a family’s home at perpetual risk. We further oppose any state or federal mandate that forces local governments to raise property taxes.

Why This Belongs in the Platform

The current MTLP platform is silent on property taxation. Section 2.4 calls for the eventual repeal of all taxation and explicitly targets the income tax; property tax — which is uniquely at odds with the libertarian conception of ownership — receives no dedicated treatment. Given the fiscal pressure that rising assessed values place on working- and middle-class Montana homeowners, this silence is a strategic gap as well as a philosophical one. This amendment fills it without creating tension with any existing plank.

Philosophical Grounding

The libertarian critique of property taxation rests on a single premise: perpetual taxation of real property is incompatible with the concept of ownership that gives property rights their meaning.

When the state can seize a home for unpaid taxes — assessed annually on the state’s own estimate of the home’s value, without regard to whether the property generates income — the relationship between the occupant and the home is not ownership in any rigorous sense. It is tenancy. The occupant pays perpetual rent to the state; in exchange, the state forbears from seizure. The occupant does not own the home. The state does.

This framing is not rhetorical. It is consistent with the Lockean theory of original appropriation by labor-mixing — the basis cited in Section 2.1 of the existing platform — and with the Austrian treatment of property rights. It is grounded in the everyday experience of Montanans who find that rising assessed values threaten their ability to remain in homes they have fully paid for. A home that can be taken for inability to pay an annual tax is not a home that is owned.

The Intermediate Measures

The amendment names five categories of interim reform, each of which could stand alone as legislation:

Constitutional limits on annual assessment increases.

Caps the growth of the tax base regardless of market fluctuations, protecting homeowners from being taxed out of their homes by a hot market.

Uniform treatment of similarly situated properties.

Similar properties should carry similar tax obligations regardless of how long the owner has held them. Systems that lock taxable values at the purchase date — such as California’s Proposition 13 — superficially protect long-term owners, but do so by shifting burden onto newer buyers and renters and by creating dramatic tax disparities between neighbors with identical homes. Libertarians should oppose this on the same grounds we oppose any state-granted privilege: the law must treat similarly situated persons similarly. Protection against tax foreclosure belongs in the homestead and vulnerable-population provisions below, not in a differential-valuation scheme.

Homestead and primary-residence protections.

Applies the lowest effective rates and the highest protections to the home a Montanan actually lives in, as distinguished from investment or commercial property.

Absolute protection for seniors, the disabled, and low-income Montanans.

No Montanan should lose their primary residence to tax foreclosure because they outlived their income or were unable to earn it. This is the strongest protection in the plank and is stated in absolute terms deliberately.

Transparent assessment, mill-levy, and appeal processes.

Ordinary Montanans should be able to understand how their property was valued, what they are being charged, and how to contest an assessment, without needing paid representation. Current processes fail this test.

Anticipated Objections

How do we fund schools and local government?

The amendment does not require the party to answer that question. It requires only the rejection of the premise that property taxation is the necessary mechanism. Education funding is addressed in Section 2.12 of the existing platform. Alternative funding mechanisms — user fees, voluntary associations, private contracts, and consumption-based taxation — are well established in libertarian policy and in practice in other jurisdictions.

Doesn’t local government need property tax revenue?

The plank explicitly identifies alternative funding mechanisms and rejects any framing that treats property taxation as necessary. Local governments in several jurisdictions operate without property taxes or with significantly reduced reliance on them.

Why no position on Georgist land-value taxation?

Land-value taxation is a legitimate debate within libertarianism and deserves its own consideration. This plank deliberately does not take a position on LVT, leaving the question open for future platform development.

Amendment II · Section 3.5

Restoration of the Condemnation of Bigotry

The Motion

Amend Section 3.5 of the Montana Libertarian Party Platform (Rights and Discrimination) by inserting the following sentence immediately before the sentence beginning “Government should neither deny nor abridge…”:

“We condemn bigotry as irrational and repugnant.”

The Amended Plank, In Context

Section 3.5 as amended would read as follows, with the restored sentence highlighted:

Libertarians embrace the concept that all people are born with certain inherent rights. We reject the idea that a natural right can ever impose an obligation upon others to fulfill that “right.” We uphold and defend the rights of every person, regardless of their race, ethnicity, or any other aspect of their identity. We condemn bigotry as irrational and repugnant. Government should neither deny nor abridge any individual’s human right based upon sex, wealth, ethnicity, creed, age, national origin, personal habits, political preference, or sexual orientation. Members of private organizations retain their rights to set whatever standards of association they deem appropriate, and individuals are free to respond with ostracism, boycotts, and other free market solutions.

Historical Context

The sentence “We condemn bigotry as irrational and repugnant” was part of the national Libertarian Party platform for approximately fifty years. It was removed at the 2022 Reno national convention. The MTLP’s 2024 platform, adopted in Big Timber, followed national practice in omitting the sentence. This amendment proposes restoring it at the Montana level.

Philosophical Grounding

Condemning bigotry as “irrational” is not imprecise rhetoric but technically precise Austrian vocabulary.

Ludwig von Mises classified racism as a form of polylogism—the doctrine that different groups of people have fundamentally different logics—and rejected it categorically. As a Jewish economist who fled the Nazis after they looted his library in Vienna, Mises saw firsthand where racial collectivism leads. He grounded his entire economic method in methodological individualism: only individuals act; nations, classes, and races have no independent agency. Bigotry—the substitution of group-membership judgment for individual judgment—is therefore irrational in the specific technical sense that it violates the foundational principle of the Austrian school.

The Mises Institute’s own publications have made this explicit. In a 2016 essay, economist Matthew McCaffrey concluded that, for Mises, racism is not just contrary to liberalism but to reason itself.

Mises himself wrote that the achievements of Western civilization justified “neither the white man’s racial self-conceit nor the political doctrines of racism” (Theory and History, 1957). He called on the Allies in 1944 to “smash Nazism” and fight until Nazi power was completely broken (Omnipotent Government, 1944). In Human Action (1949) he demonstrated systematically that racist doctrines of group difference can never be substantiated—racists, he wrote, have “never ventured to demonstrate precisely in what the logic of the Aryans differs from the logic of the non-Aryans.”

If we are libertarians in the Austrian tradition—if methodological individualism means anything to us—then condemning bigotry as irrational is not foreign language. It is our language.

What This Amendment Does and Does Not Do

This amendment does not alter the party’s position on freedom of association. The sentence immediately following—which remains intact—explicitly protects the right of private organizations to set their own standards of association.

Libertarians routinely distinguish between what we would legally permit and what we would morally endorse. The 2024 MTLP platform already does this explicitly in Section 1.0: “our support of an individual’s right to make choices in life does not mean that we necessarily approve or disapprove of those choices.” This amendment is consistent with that practice.

Anticipated Objections

Platforms should address government action, not private morality.

The current MTLP platform already contains extensive non-governmental content. The Preamble welcomes the diversity that freedom brings. Section 3.3 condemns terrorism by non-state actors. Section 1.0 explicitly addresses approval or disapproval of private choices. Singling out this one sentence for removal on these grounds is selective.

The current positive language covers the same ground.

It does not. “We uphold and defend the rights of every person” describes what the party and government will do. It is silent on bigotry as a phenomenon. A racist can read the current Section 3.5 and find nothing to disagree with. The condemnation sentence does distinct moral work that the positive language cannot do.

One person’s bigotry is another’s freedom of association.

The amendment changes nothing about freedom of association. The next sentence in the plank—which stays intact—explicitly protects it. Condemning bigotry morally while defending its legal permissibility is a fundamental libertarian distinction, not a contradiction.

The 2022 removal was outreach to the broader liberty movement.

That tactical argument has now been tested for three years. National Libertarian Party membership has declined. Revenue has declined. State affiliates in Virginia, New Mexico, and Massachusetts have disaffiliated. Montana is not obligated to follow a strategy whose empirical results have been measurably negative.

  • Mises, Ludwig von. Human Action: A Treatise on Economics. 1949.
  • Mises, Ludwig von. Theory and History: An Interpretation of Social and Economic Evolution. 1957.
  • McCaffrey, Matthew. “Mises on the Battle between Liberalism and Racism.” Mises Wire, September 29, 2016. mises.org.

Amendment III · Section 1.3

Privacy and Modern Surveillance

The Motion

Amend Section 1.3 (Privacy) of the Montana Libertarian Party Platform to read as follows, replacing the current text in its entirety:

Libertarians advocate individual privacy and government transparency. We are committed to ending government’s practice of spying on everyone. We support the rights recognized by the Fourth Amendment to be secure in our persons, homes, property, and communications. Protection from unreasonable search and seizure should include records held by third parties — such as email, medical, library, and financial records, and the location, communication, and transaction metadata held by service providers of any kind.

These protections apply regardless of the technology used to circumvent them. We oppose the mass collection of information about persons who are not subjects of particularized suspicion — including through automated license plate readers, cell-site simulators, biometric and facial recognition databases, drone and aerial surveillance, and persistent monitoring of public spaces. We specifically oppose “reverse warrants,” including geofence warrants and keyword search warrants, which invert the Fourth Amendment’s requirement of individualized probable cause by treating everyone in a location or everyone who searched a term as a potential suspect. We likewise oppose the issuance of government-controlled digital currencies, which by design make every transaction visible to the state; the right to use cash, cryptocurrency, and any other mutually agreeable means of exchange — including privacy-preserving alternatives — must be preserved.

Government must not be permitted to purchase from private data brokers any information it would otherwise require a warrant to compel; the Fourth Amendment does not have a commercial exception. We oppose parallel construction — the practice of using surveillance-derived intelligence to guide investigations while concealing the surveillance origin from courts and defendants. We oppose any government effort to mandate backdoors, key escrow, or the deliberate weakening of encryption; strong encryption is essential to the right to be secure in one’s communications.

Why This Expansion

The current Section 1.3 is a solid 2015-era plank. Essentially every specific surveillance technology or practice that has become routine in the decade since is either unaddressed or only implicitly covered. Automated license plate readers blanket Montana towns. Cell-site simulators, facial recognition databases, drone surveillance, geofence warrants, and keyword warrants are routine tools for local, state, and federal law enforcement. Commercial data brokers openly sell government agencies information the Fourth Amendment would otherwise require a warrant to compel. Parallel construction allows surveillance-derived intelligence to guide prosecutions without ever appearing in the evidentiary record.

This amendment is not a departure from the existing plank. It is an extension of a principle already stated — that “protection from unreasonable search and seizure should include records held by third parties” — into the specific technologies and practices that have emerged to circumvent it.

Philosophical Grounding

Financial privacy is inseparable from other civil liberties. Surveillance of transactions is surveillance of association (whom you pay), belief (what you donate to), and action (what you do, buy, and attend). A government that can see every transaction can, in practice, regulate every relationship and silence any dissent by threatening commerce. This is not speculative; it is the demonstrated logic of state-controlled payment systems worldwide.

This is why central bank digital currencies belong in the privacy plank rather than the monetary plank. Section 2.7 already handles the monetary questions — commodity money, free banking, mutually agreeable media of exchange. The objection to CBDCs is not that they are an alternative form of money; it is that they are surveillance infrastructure wearing money as a costume. Addressing them as a surveillance issue frames the concern correctly.

CBDCs Are Not Cryptocurrency

A central bank digital currency is the opposite of cryptocurrency in structure and in purpose. Cryptocurrency is decentralized, permissionless, and — in privacy-preserving variants — pseudonymous by design. A CBDC is centralized, permissioned, and surveillable by design, with transactions visible to and programmable by the issuing authority. The two are not variations on the same theme; they are adversaries. This plank opposes one while affirming the right to use the other.

The Montana Advantage

Article II, Section 10 of the Montana Constitution guarantees: “The right of individual privacy is essential to the well-being of a free society and shall not be infringed without the showing of a compelling state interest.” This is a stronger protection than exists under the Fourth Amendment alone and has been enforced by Montana courts as a higher bar than the federal standard. Montana has also passed some of the strongest state-level digital privacy legislation in recent years.

This is a strategic asset. Montana is positioned to lead the nation on surveillance limits rather than follow. The MTLP should consistently frame surveillance reform as Montana leadership on a principle Montana’s own constitution already endorses — not as oppositional politics.

Platform Principles vs. Legislative Asks

This amendment articulates principles. Several specific legislative asks follow from it and should be pursued as separate work:

Close the data broker loophole at the state level.

A Montana statute prohibiting state and local agencies from purchasing from commercial brokers information that would otherwise require a warrant. Model language exists at the federal level (the Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act) and in other states.

Limit retention and sharing of ALPR data.

Montana-specific legislation capping retention periods for automated license plate reader data collected by state and local agencies, and restricting inter-agency and commercial sharing.

Prohibit reverse warrants.

Legislation barring Montana state and local law enforcement from obtaining or executing geofence warrants and keyword search warrants.

Require audit and transparency for surveillance programs.

Legislation requiring public inventory, retention policies, annual reporting, and independent audit of state and local surveillance programs.

These are bills to be taken to legislators, not platform text. Senator Daniel Zolnikov has a strong record on digital privacy and is the obvious point of contact for drafting and introducing this legislation.

Anticipated Objections

This plank is too long.

It is denser than some existing planks but comparable to others — the current 2.1, 2.7, and 3.3 are all similar in length or longer. Surveillance is a multi-faceted issue, and a plank that only articulates abstract privacy commitments without naming the practices being opposed is a plank that doesn’t bind the party to anything in particular.

Why list specific technologies that will be obsolete in ten years?

Paragraph 2 opens with the principle: protections apply regardless of the technology used to circumvent them. The enumerated technologies are illustrative of the present case, not a limiting list. Stating the principle and naming current examples is how platform planks have always worked — Section 1.9 names specific firearm-related categories, Section 2.4 names the Internal Revenue Service, and so on.

Isn’t the data broker language legislation?

The principle — that government cannot purchase what it would need a warrant to compel — is a principle. The specific statutory implementation is legislation. The plank states the principle; the bill (proposed separately) implements it. This division is consistent with how other platform planks operate.

Opposition to CBDCs reads as opposition to digital currency generally.

The plank explicitly distinguishes. Paragraph 2 affirms the right to use cryptocurrency, including privacy-preserving variants. The opposition is specifically to government-controlled digital currency, named as such. The commentary above makes the structural contrast between CBDCs and cryptocurrency explicit.

Parallel construction is too technical for a platform.

Parallel construction is the single largest accountability failure in modern surveillance practice. If the platform does not name it, the platform is committing to a Fourth Amendment that applies only until a prosecutor decides to clean up the trail. It belongs in the plank.

Amendment IV · Section 1.7

Civil Asset Forfeiture

The Motion

Amend Section 1.7 (Crime and Justice) of the Montana Libertarian Party Platform by adding the following after the existing sentence on qualified immunity:

We oppose civil asset forfeiture as fundamentally incompatible with due process and the presumption of innocence. No property — whether cash, vehicle, home, or any other asset — shall be taken by the state except upon conviction of its owner of a specific crime, and only to an extent proportionate to that crime. We oppose the use of federal “equitable sharing” programs by Montana state and local agencies to circumvent state-level protections against forfeiture. Agencies that seize property shall derive no financial benefit from those seizures; proceeds of lawful forfeitures must flow to general funds, not to the seizing agency.

Why This Consolidation

The current MTLP platform mentions civil asset forfeiture only once, as a list item in Section 2.1 (Aggression, Property, and Contract). That list characterizes forfeiture as an abridgement of property rights but says nothing substantive about what we propose to do about it. Section 1.7 (Crime and Justice), which addresses related criminal-justice concerns at length — qualified immunity, over-charging, plea bargaining, jury nullification — is silent on forfeiture entirely.

This amendment does not remove the reference from 2.1 (it legitimately belongs in a property rights list) but places the substantive treatment in 1.7, where civil asset forfeiture actually lives as a policy matter. Forfeiture is a criminal-justice practice that happens to implicate property rights; it is not a property-rights abstraction that happens to involve law enforcement.

Philosophical Grounding

Civil asset forfeiture inverts the presumption of innocence. In ordinary criminal procedure, the state must prove the defendant’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt before imposing punishment. In civil forfeiture, the state seizes property on suspicion, and the owner must affirmatively prove that the property was not involved in wrongdoing — often without ever being charged with a crime themselves.

The legal fiction that makes this possible is the in rem proceeding: the state sues the property rather than the owner. Case names like United States v. $124,700 in U.S. Currency are not satire; they are how the practice operates in court. Treating property as capable of being guilty of a crime, and requiring its owner to prove otherwise, is incompatible with any serious theory of due process.

The practice also creates perverse incentives. When seizing agencies keep the proceeds of forfeited property, the decision to pursue forfeiture becomes a budgetary decision rather than a law-enforcement one. The affected population skews heavily toward those who cannot afford the extended litigation required to recover their own property. Policing for profit is not a caricature; it is what the incentive structure produces.

The Montana Context

Montana already led on this issue. In 2015, the legislature passed HB 463, sponsored by Rep. Kelly McCarthy (D-Billings) and co-sponsored by then-Rep. Daniel Zolnikov (R-Billings). The law required a criminal conviction before most forfeitures, raised the standard of proof linking property to crime to “clear and convincing evidence,” and shifted the innocent-owner burden onto the government. The Institute for Justice called it the “gold standard” — second only to New Mexico’s “platinum standard.”

Two gaps remain, and this amendment addresses both.

First, the federal equitable sharing loophole. When Montana law enforcement partners with a federal agency on a case, property can be forfeited under federal rules — which do not require conviction — and up to 80% of the proceeds flow back to the Montana agency. This mechanism allows state and local agencies to bypass Montana’s own conviction requirement. New Mexico closed this loophole when it enacted its reforms; Montana did not. The plank calls for closing it.

Second, the profit incentive. Montana law permits up to 100% of forfeiture proceeds to flow to law enforcement. This structural incentive — distinct from the conviction requirement — is what drives the “policing for profit” pattern even where conviction is required. The plank calls for routing proceeds to general funds instead, removing budgetary gain as a motive.

The framing of this amendment should be explicit: Montana led the nation on forfeiture reform in 2015. This finishes the job.

Platform Principles vs. Legislative Asks

This amendment states principles. The implementing legislation is straightforward and has close precedent:

Close the equitable sharing loophole.

A statute prohibiting Montana state and local agencies from transferring seized property to federal agencies for federal forfeiture, and from receiving proceeds of federal forfeitures involving such transfers, except where federal forfeiture is pursued in connection with an actual federal criminal prosecution. New Mexico’s 2015 reform provides model language.

Route forfeiture proceeds to general funds.

A statute redirecting the proceeds of lawful forfeitures from seizing agencies to the state general fund. Again, New Mexico’s 2015 reform is the model.

Both are bills to be taken to legislators, not platform text. Senator Daniel Zolnikov, as co-sponsor of the 2015 reform, is the obvious point of contact for drafting and introducing the follow-up legislation — and has a demonstrated history of delivering on this issue.

Anticipated Objections

We need civil asset forfeiture to disrupt drug trafficking and organized crime.

The amendment does not abolish forfeiture. It requires that forfeiture follow criminal conviction — which is exactly what Montana law already requires in most cases under HB 463, and which remains fully available as a tool against actual criminals. The objection conflates criminal forfeiture (post-conviction, fine) with civil forfeiture (pre-conviction or no-conviction, opposed).

Won’t removing the profit incentive hurt law enforcement budgets?

Yes, and that is the point. The proposition that an agency’s budget should depend on its success at seizing property from citizens creates exactly the kind of incentive a principled libertarian party should oppose. Law enforcement funding is a legitimate topic for the legislature and for voters. It should not be a function of how much property the agency can take.

Isn’t this already covered in 2.1?

Only in the sense that “we oppose it” is implied by its inclusion in a list of abridgements of rights. The platform does not explain what we oppose, what we would do about it, or what we would preserve. A one-word list entry is not a substantive position.

Why name the federal equitable sharing loophole specifically?

Because it is the documented primary mechanism by which state-level forfeiture reforms are circumvented, and because Montana’s 2015 reform left it open. Naming it specifically ensures the plank addresses the actual current gap, not a theoretical one.
